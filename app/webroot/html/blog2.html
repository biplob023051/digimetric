  • Hiring Bad: The reasons for, costs of, and solution to bad hires
    Aug 14, 2014 by Mark vincent
    While the economy is slowly improving, now more than ever businesses must find ways to reduce financial insecurity and promote internal stability. Hiring quality employees who possess dependable skills is an important ingredient in this recipe for long-term sustainable growth. Firms have an interest in identifying and obtaining the best talent available in the market, but sometimes new hires fail when employee outcomes do not match company expectations. Why do bad hires occur?


    According to a 2012 CareerBuilder survey, the main reason companies make bad hires (expressed by 42% of respondents) is because they felt they “needed to act quickly.” Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) in particular are prone to rush the hiring process. Jeff Freeland, CEO of recruiting start-up Jobularity, explains: “Small business just doesn't have enough time, energy, or resources to react quickly enough to the demands of their customers. As a result, quick decisions are made on critical hires, which results in the poor fit.” As the old saying goes, “haste makes waste.” So what are the costs associated with bad hires?


    The financial impact of a bad hire is substantial. The National Business Research Institute (NBRI) identified five kinds of direct costs associated with a bad hire—productivity losses, training costs, HR staff costs, interviewing costs, and employment advertisements. Indirect costs also include negative impact on client relations, decrease in sales, and affected employee morale. In the US, 27% of employers responding to a 2013 CareerBuilder survey stated that a bad hire cost their business over $50,000. That figure is likely a conservative estimate. For higher paying jobs, the NBRI reports that costs may range from more than $150,000 to nearly $300,000. Tony Hsieh, CEO of Zappos, famously claimed that bad hires have cost his company “well over $100 million.” Given the high cost of poor hiring, firms would be well advised to pursue strategies designed to mitigate the risks associated with recruiting talent. What can businesses do to avoid suffering these unnecessary hits to their bottom line?


    The answer is that firms can hire smarter. The most significant characteristic of a bad hire, as described by 67% of employers participating in the 2012 CareerBuilder survey, is that the “employee didn’t produce the proper quality of work.” Aside from rushing the hiring process, the leading reason businesses make bad hires, the next key contributor to this systematic problem identified in the same survey is “insufficient talent intelligence.” Together, these findings suggest that the better prepared companies are to assess the actual abilities of potential employees (as opposed to the alleged abilities featured on a resume), the less likely they are to experience financial losses incurred from not hiring the right person for the job. This means that acquiring talent requires sophisticated analysis of a candidate’s capacity to perform tasks specific to a given position.


    Although the need to hire quickly, especially among SMEs, is not likely to abate soon, the gap between alleged and actual talent can be closed through rigorous measurement of potential employees’ skills and delivering the results of these assessments to hiring managers in the form of easily understood analytics. With better talent intelligence technology in place, companies can stop hiring bad and start hiring smarter.
